Real-world cases · Documented practice

What's already being done.

Eight cases, two contexts. The first four are the closest practical precursor to a customer digital twin in auto insurance: telematics-driven usage-based insurance (UBI) programs that have been live in Canada and the United States for over a decade. The next four are mature digital twin implementations in industries where the technology is no longer emerging — manufacturing, energy, smart buildings, automotive engineering. Read the caveat at the end.

Auto insurance · telematics & UBI in North America

The closest precursor — and what it tells us

Telematics-based UBI is not a digital twin in the strict sense. A UBI program collects driving signals and applies them to a discount formula; a digital twin maintains a continuously-updated virtual model of the customer that simulates outcomes under different conditions. But UBI is the most data-rich, customer-personalized practice that exists in this market today, and it is what any future digital-twin offering will be compared against.

United States · launched 2010s

Progressive Snapshot

A telematics program that tracks braking, acceleration, night driving, miles driven, and phone use through a smartphone app or plug-in device. Discounts apply at signup and at renewal based on observed behavior.

Result: Approximately four out of five customers earn a discount; about half earn a double-digit discount. About 20% see an increased premium. Progressive reports more than $1.2 billion in cumulative Snapshot discounts and an average $322/year saving for renewing customers. Roughly 20% of drivers see a premium increase.

Sources: Progressive ↗ · CIO interview ↗

United States · Drivewise

Allstate Drivewise

App-based UBI program with a one-way discount structure: rates can stay flat or go down, but never increase based on the data. Allstate advertises potential savings up to 40%.

Result: Realistic savings cluster between 7–15% for typical drivers; Consumer Reports' median is around $324/year. Allstate's own April 2024 data: Drivewise customers were 25% less likely to be involved in a serious collision than non-participants — an externality worth noting.

Sources: Allstate ↗ · Bankrate review ↗

Canada · Ontario, NS, NB, QC

Intact Insurance — my Drive

Canada's largest P&C insurer launched its smartphone-based UBI program in Ontario in 2013. Three behavioral inputs: speed, focus (phone distraction), and smoothness (acceleration / braking). The app is built on Cambridge Mobile Telematics technology.

Result: 10% enrolment discount on signup; up to 25–30% off at renewal for safe driving. Premium can adjust up or down at the end of the initial 6-month observation period. Available in Ontario, Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, and Quebec — directly applicable to this project's geography.

Sources: Intact ↗ · ThinkInsure review ↗

Canada · QC, ON

Desjardins — Ajusto

Launched 2013 in Ontario and Quebec. Among the earliest 100% mobile-only UBI programs in Canada (no plug-in device required). Tracks driving habits (speed, acceleration, hard braking, phone distraction) and behavior (distance, time of day, destinations).

Result: 10% sign-up discount for the first 180 days; thereafter, safe driving can lower premiums by up to 25%, while riskier driving can increase them by up to 20%. The bidirectional structure (unlike Allstate's) is more honest about how a real digital twin would operate — and is what generated the most public privacy debate (CBC News coverage, 2015).

Sources: Desjardins ↗ · CBC News ↗

Adjacent industries · mature digital twins

Where the technology is no longer emerging

Outside of insurance, digital twins have a decade of operational evidence. The four cases below were chosen because they have public performance data, not because they are the most-cited in vendor marketing. Each illustrates a different facet of the technology that will eventually transfer to customer-side modelling in financial services.

Manufacturing · Germany

Siemens — Electronics Works Amberg

A digital twin of an entire production line, in operation for over a decade. Engineers test what-if scenarios virtually without halting production. The plant produces over 1,000 SIMATIC controller variants per day, one product per second.

Result: 99.9% quality level maintained. Defect rate reduced by over 60%. Production volume increased 13× since 1990 with no expansion of the 10,000 m² floor and almost no change in headcount. Energy use down 20%. The reference case in industrial DT literature.

Sources: Siemens (Amberg) ↗ · Siemens Insights ↗

Energy · global

GE — Digital Wind Farm

Per-turbine digital twins built on GE's Predix platform. Each twin runs up to 20 different operating configurations to find the optimal pad-level setup for that specific turbine, then keeps learning from real-time sensor data.

Result: GE reports up to 20% boost in wind farm energy production, with per-turbine MWh output gains in the 5–7% range and turbine profitability up to 20% higher per unit. Estimated industry-wide value at $50 billion. The most cited operational-DT case outside manufacturing.

Sources: GE press release ↗ · HBR (sponsor content) ↗

Smart buildings · platform

Microsoft — Azure Digital Twins

A platform-as-a-service offering for modelling physical environments and assets. Used internally by Microsoft for its own smart-building experiences (pathfinding, hot-desking) and externally by partners including Willow, Johnson Controls, Bosch, Bentley Systems, Ansys and Samsung.

Result: Most relevant signal for this project: the platform generalizes the digital-twin metaphor beyond a single use case. The same primitives (DTDL ontology, twin graph, change feed) that model a building can model a customer relationship.

Sources: Azure Digital Twins ↗ · Microsoft Inside Track ↗

Automotive · United States

Tesla — vehicle & battery twins

Tesla maintains a digital twin of every vehicle it has sold. Sensor data uploads continuously to the cloud, feeding models of battery state, motor function, and Autopilot behavior. Software updates are pre-validated against the population of twins before being pushed.

Result: Battery digital twins enable real-time charging-cycle optimization and health monitoring. Published research suggests battery efficiency gains of up to 30% and a 20% per-kWh cell cost reduction with mature twin implementations. The clearest current example of a per-customer (per-vehicle) operational twin at scale.

Sources: Cloudflight analysis ↗ · arXiv 2206.08080 (battery DT) ↗

Read this honestly

A true customer digital twin in auto insurance does not yet exist at scale.

Every auto insurance case above is a usage-based insurance program, not a digital twin in the literature's sense. UBI feeds a discount formula; a digital twin maintains a continuously-updated model of the individual and simulates outcomes under counterfactual conditions. The two share plumbing — telematics, behavioral data, smartphone apps — but they differ in what the model is for.

Every adjacent-industry case is mature, but not customer-side. Siemens models a factory; GE models a turbine; Microsoft models a building; Tesla models a vehicle. None of them models a person whose insurance premium depends on the model's output, with the latter knowing it. Bringing the technology over the line into customer twins surfaces questions the industrial cases have not had to answer: meaningful consent, the right to inspect one's own twin, the right to challenge what the twin says.

Which is why the simulator in this project is a research prototype. It is built deliberately to make those questions visible — what changes when a twin replaces a segment, what the customer sees, what the marketing message can honestly say. Try it, then read the methodology for what it does and does not claim.
